Philip Resnik's Academic Geneology

Noah Smith was kind enough to share a perl script with me that automatically scrapes academic geneologies from The Mathematics Geneology Project. I've fleshed the info out a bit going back a few generations in a table below with some links and notes. But that's not the end of it: scroll down past the table for more distant ancestry, which is pretty mind-blowing.

The TL;DR is that I've been influenced by three main lines of intellectual descent.

  • One line, through my advisor Aravind Joshi, is a lineage primarily in mathematics. This purer form of math has not been as strong an influence on my intellectual life because Aravind himself, by the time I had the good fortune to work with him, had long since shifted his focus away from that to lines of thinking in cognitive science influenced by his interactions with Zelig Harris.

  • I trace the second line through Zelig Harris, not only because of the clear intellectual line-of-descent influence on Aravind as my advisor, but also because Lila Gleitman, while not my advisor, was a mentor at Penn and one of the strongest influences on how I think about things, and she also engaged signficantly with Harris. A Harris-based line traces back through generations of language (philology) and theology scholars, though interestingly there seems to be a definite theme of people who were taking a more rationalist than religious angle.

  • Finally, also through Harris, there is a fascinating line of descent that runs through astronomy and captures the historical progression from religious and philosophical thinking to scientific thinking.

Of course it's easy to rationalize post hoc, but all this feels to me like it makes good sense: I'm descended from a combination of mathematics (well, computation, but the two go hand in hand; my undergrad class entering in Fall 1983 was the first one that could declare CS as a major, rather than studying it as a part of applied math), and language, and empirically driven natural sciences. Not just one, but all three. That's pretty cool.

Aravind Krishna Joshi Seymour Sherman 1960 University of Pennsylvania Some Applications of Information Theory and a Generalization of Normalized Entropy Key figure in computational linguistics; co-founded UPenn's Institute for Research in Cognitive Science. I (along with a lot of others, I think) believed that Zelig Harris was Joshi's advisor. However David Chiang dug into this, actually inter-library-loaning Joshi's dissertation, and found that the advisor of record was actually Seymour Sherman. I followed up with Mitch Marcus to get the fuller story. In a nutshell, Mitch reports that Aravind's early work was entirely about information theory/communication theory and, except for one paper in 1958, all of his papers until 1964 were in that area. But then, influenced by Harris, Aravind abandoned information theory per se and pursued the computational work that his interaction with Harris led to. Mitch notes that the birth of cognitive science at Penn was the interaction among the grad students working for Harris, and not Harris himself --- Aravind, Lila, and Carol Chomsky (who did her undergrad at Penn and her PhD at Harvard).

  • Johann Gottfried Eichorn, described as the "founder of modern Old Testament Criticism", who believed that all the supernatural events in the Old Testament happened by natural principles and "did not perceive in the biblical books any religious ideas of much importance for modern times; they interested him merely historically and for the light they cast upon antiquity".

  • Wilhelm Gesenius, who "takes much of the credit for having freed Semitic philology from the trammels of theological and religious prepossession, and for inaugurating the strictly scientific (and comparative) method which has since been so fruitful".

  • Johann Gottfried Jakob Hermann, a classicist I include here because I love the fact that one of the positions he held was "professor of eloquence".

  • Georg Wilhelm Hegel -- Hegel!!

  • Adriaan Heereboord, a Dutch philosopher and logician who liked the "new thinking" of Descartes, was influenced by Francis Bacon, and was "a combative drinker" whose private life was "the subject of pamphlets".

  • Johannes Buxtorf the Younger, who did work on ancient Hebrew; he was the son of a celebrated Hebraist (Buxtorf the Elder), who is referred to as ""Master of the Rabbis" and "was largely responsible for transforming Hebrew studies from an amateur hobby into an established academic discipline in the early seventeenth century"; Buxtorf the Younger completed a number of his father's works, including a Hebrew lexicon and concordance (hey look, corpus data!).

  • Tycho Brahe (!) -- described as "the first competent mind in modern astronomy to feel ardently the passion for exact empirical facts" and "the last of the major astronomers to work without telescopes".

  • Johannes Kepler (!!)

  • Nicolaus Copernicus (!!) Holy crap. I knew there was a reason I did a report on him in 3rd grade.

  • Erasmus (!!!) Him too, I forget what grade. And this is through a different lineage from Kepler and Brahe.

  • Thomas Cranmer Ok, at this point I'm just going to stop with the exclamation points. (I did think that Cranmer was one of the most interesting characters in Wolf Hall...)

  • Martin Luther and Philipp Melancthon. Ok, now, seriously? This is just getting silly.

  • Not so silly that I could fail to include William of Ockham, who, incidentally, was the first philosopher to dig into the notion of "mental language". So I've got cognitive science in my heredity dating all the way back to the 1300s!
