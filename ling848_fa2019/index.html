Overview of Course Goals

This is an interdisciplinary seminar designed to engage people who are interested in language and the brain from the perspectives of computational linguistics, psycholinguistics, and cognitive neuroscience. It is organized as a topics-oriented seminar focused on readings and discussion.

The topics we look at will be motivated by the following questions:

  • How can greater understanding of and attention to neuroscience -- consideration of the implementation level as well as algorithms and representations -- improve our understanding of language from a computational perspective?

  • How can the thought process and methods from computational linguistics contribute to an improved understanding of language in the brain?

  • What specific challenges does language pose to current theories of general mechanisms in computational neuroscience.

Grading

This seminar will mainly involve readings and discussion in class and on Piazza.

  • Reaction/discussion essays (20%).

    For all attending students (whether registered, auditing, or otherwise), you're required to do the readings and, no later than the Monday night before class, write a post to the class discussion board containing (in a single post):

    • A summary/synthesis of your “takeaways” from the previous class (incl. any post-class discussion that was had that you think was valuable), on the course’s Piazza discussion page
    • A short reaction-paper to the coming week’s readings, to help get the in-class discussion started

      • Can include: assumptions you encountered that are unfamiliar to you; things you didn’t understand; things you did understand that you strongly agree/disagree with.

  • Participation (30%). This includes:

    • Participating on the class discussion board (see above). The Monday due date for reaction writeups is designed so that people can get some discussion going online ahead of class, so that in class we're already ramped up.

    • Presentations/leading of discussion in class. Everyone will do this a few times during the semester, and I'd like that to be true even for people who are not there for a grade. Please indicate your availability at https://doodle.com/poll/4as3nz8p5vxdy8ia.

    • Participating actively in discussions during class.

  • Term paper or project (50%).

    About halfway through the semester, we will discuss possible papers or projects. These projects are due on Friday December 13, 2019. We may use the class time on December 4th for preliminary presentations/discussion, so plan ahead.

Cheating and collaboration. What you represent as your own work must be your own work. However, talking with one another to understand the material better is strongly encouraged. Recognizing the distinction between cheating and cooperation is very important. If you simply copy someone else's work, you are cheating. If you let someone else copy your work, you are cheating. If someone dictates something to you, you are cheating. Everything you hand in must be in your own words, and based on your own understanding. If someone helps you understand a problem or issue during a high-level discussion, you are not cheating. If you work collaboratively with explicit permission from the instructor, you are not cheating. I strongly encourage students to help one another understand the material presented in class, in the readings, and general issues relevant to any assignments. Any student who is caught cheating will be given an F in the course and referred to the University Student Behavior Committee. Please don't take that chance -- if you're having trouble understanding the material, or if you need some help clarifying what is ok to do and what is not, please let me know and I will be more than happy to help.

Names and Pronouns. Many people might go by a name in daily life that is different from their legal name. In this classroom, we seek to refer to people by the names that they go by. Pronouns can be a way to affirm someone's gender identity, but they can also be unrelated to a person's identity. They are simply a public way in which people are referred to in place of their name (e.g. "he" or "she" or "they" or "ze" or something else). In this classroom, you are invited (if you want to) to share what pronouns you go by, and we seek to refer to people using the pronouns that they share. The pronouns someone indicates are not necessarily indicative of their gender identity. Visit trans.umd.edu to learn more.
